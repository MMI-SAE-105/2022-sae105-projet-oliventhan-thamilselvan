A ray of hope

Sri Lanka plunged into military conflict between the government and the separatist Tamil group, the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) The peace process was exclusively focused on two parties: the government, then led by Ranil Wickremesinghe of the United National Party (UNP), and the LTTE.

The humanitarian crisis

The humanitarian crisis in the north east is critical, with more than 200,000 fleeing their homes during the year. The international community has a key role to play in restraining both sides and pushing for serious discussion of a political settlement. Sri Lanka more than ever before needs international engagement that is critical and sustained. Until attitudes change on both sides, the immediate prospect is for worsening violence.

Failure at finding peace”

Talks in February and October 2006 failed to restart discussion of a political settlement. The renewal of conflict under the administration of President Mahinda Rajapakse makes any political settlement more difficult. There is little evidence that either side can win militarily. A 2002 ceasefire, negotiated with Norway’s help, remains intact on paper but is flouted on the ground with increasing regularity and frequent brutality. Human rights abuses and political killings are carried out with impunity by the governement.

Karma at it's finest

Following months of escalating protests, and the May resignation of his brother Prime Minister Mahinda Rajapaksa, Sri Lankan President Gotabaya Rajapaksa fled the country on July 13. The country’s economy has hit rock bottom as it defaulted on international loans and is facing rampant fuel and food shortages, and the government imposed a state of emergency. The ex-president’s flight from the country leaves the government in further disarray.

Peace may finally return in the country after the governement that was in power during the genocide got demolished and kicked out the country by all people of Sri Lanka.
